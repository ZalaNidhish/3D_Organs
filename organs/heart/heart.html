Structure Of Heart

The human heart is a mesodermally derived organ located in the mediastinum of the thoracic cavity, slightly tilted towards the left. It is a muscular, four-chambered, hollow organ roughly the size of a closed fist. Its primary function is to circulate blood throughout the body and lungs.

The heart is enclosed in a double-layered membranous sac called the pericardium. The outer layer is fibrous, while the inner layer is serous and secretes pericardial fluid. This fluid acts as a lubricant and reduces friction during heartbeats.

Internally, the heart is divided into four chambers: two atria (auricles) on the upper side and two ventricles on the lower side. The atria are thin-walled, while the ventricles have thick muscular walls, with the left ventricle being the thickest to withstand high pressure and pump blood to the entire body.

The right and left halves of the heart are separated by a thick wall called the septum. The septum between the atria is the interatrial septum, and the one between the ventricles is the interventricular septum. The atria and ventricles on each side are separated by the atrioventricular septum, which contains valves to prevent backflow of blood.

The right atrium receives deoxygenated blood from the body via two large veins: the superior vena cava and the inferior vena cava. It also receives blood from the heart wall through the coronary sinus. The left atrium receives oxygenated blood from the lungs through four pulmonary veins.

From the right atrium, blood flows into the right ventricle through the tricuspid valve, a valve with three cusps. Blood from the left atrium flows into the left ventricle through the bicuspid valve (also known as the mitral valve), which has two cusps. These atrioventricular valves prevent the backflow of blood into the atria during ventricular contraction.

The right ventricle pumps blood into the pulmonary artery through a semilunar valve, sending it to the lungs for oxygenation. The left ventricle pumps oxygenated blood into the aorta through another semilunar valve to distribute it throughout the body. These semilunar valves also prevent backflow into the ventricles.

The heart's muscular wall is made of a specialized type of muscle tissue called cardiac muscle, which is involuntary, striated, and capable of rhythmic contractions. The wall has three layers: the inner endocardium, the middle thick myocardium, and the outer epicardium.

The heart functions as a double pump. The right side of the heart handles pulmonary circulation, pumping deoxygenated blood to the lungs, while the left side manages systemic circulation, pumping oxygenated blood to the body. This results in a complete double circulation, which is a characteristic feature of birds and mammals, ensuring separation of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood.

The opening and closing of heart valves and the rhythmic contraction and relaxation of heart chambers ensure unidirectional flow of blood. This coordinated functioning is vital for efficient circulation and is regulated by the conducting system of the heart, including the Sinoatrial (SA) node, Atrioventricular (AV) node, Bundle of His, and Purkinje fibers. Though this system is mainly discussed under heartbeat, its structural elements are embedded in the heart tissue.

Working Of Heart

The functioning of the heart involves a sequential and rhythmic contraction and relaxation of the atria and ventricles to ensure continuous, unidirectional flow of blood. This rhythmic contraction is known as the cardiac cycle, which occurs once every heartbeat and lasts approximately 0.8 seconds.

A single cardiac cycle consists of three main phases: atrial systole, ventricular systole, and joint diastole. During atrial systole, the atria contract and pump blood into the ventricles. This is followed by ventricular systole, in which the ventricles contract and pump blood into the pulmonary artery and aorta. Finally, in joint diastole, both the atria and ventricles relax, allowing blood to enter the atria again.

The heart's rhythmic activity is initiated and regulated by the nodal tissue, which includes the Sinoatrial (SA) node, Atrioventricular (AV) node, Bundle of His, and Purkinje fibers. The SA node, located in the right atrium, is called the pacemaker of the heart because it generates the maximum number of action potentials, typically about 70â€“75 per minute.

The electrical impulse generated by the SA node spreads over the atria, causing them to contract. It then reaches the AV node, which is located between the atria and ventricles. The AV node passes the impulse to the ventricles via the AV bundle (Bundle of His), which branches into the right and left bundle branches and then into Purkinje fibers that stimulate ventricular contraction.

This conduction pathway ensures that the atria contract a fraction of a second before the ventricles, allowing complete filling of ventricles before they contract. This coordination maintains the efficiency of blood circulation and prevents mixing of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood.

The heart sounds heard during auscultation are produced due to the closure of valves. The first heart sound "lub" is caused by the closure of the atrioventricular valves at the beginning of ventricular systole. The second heart sound "dub" is produced by the closure of the semilunar valves at the beginning of ventricular diastole.

The amount of blood pumped by each ventricle per minute is called cardiac output. In a healthy adult, the average cardiac output is approximately 5000 mL (5 litres) per minute. It is the product of the heart rate and stroke volume (the amount of blood pumped per beat).

The heart's activity is myogenic in nature, meaning it originates from the cardiac muscles themselves and is not dependent on nervous stimuli. However, the autonomic nervous system (ANS) modulates the rate and force of heartbeat. The sympathetic system increases heart rate and cardiac output, while the parasympathetic (vagus nerve) decreases it.

The heart is also influenced by hormones such as adrenaline and noradrenaline, which increase heart rate during stress or emergency situations, allowing the body to respond more effectively.
